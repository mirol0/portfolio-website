Traverse

OVERVIEW

CHALLENGE

Create a native app for both iOS and Android that solves a common problem in the mountain biking community: a significant need for an information hub.

GOAL

The main goals for this project were to understand the mountain bike industry and what exists in the space, and then create an engaging product that benefits the target user. I relied on my husband and his coworks at The Pro's Closet, a Boulder, CO bike company, for research materials and feedback.

ROLE

As the sole UX/UI designer, I conducted through research on mountain bike-related apps, and created a competitive analysis of to large indirect competitors. From there, I applied human-centered design thinking principles to create a design solution. I created rapid prototypes, using pen and paper and later, Adobe XD, to test and refine my design along the way.

RESULT

The result is a final product that understands its strengths. The personalized features are what set it apart from other similar apps, and this focus on easing pain points makes it accessbile engaging for mountain bikers.

UX Research and Design

COMPETITIVE ANALYSIS

While a lot of content exists for biking generally-speaking, I was surprised at just how little is out there for mountain biking. That is why I chose to do a competitive analyis with indirect competitors, like Trailforks and MTB Project.

USER RESEARCH

With a better understanding of what currently exists in the app space, it was now time to talk with mountain bikers directly. For this, I relied on 3 employees of The Pro's Closet, a local bike company where I live, for industry insight and possible pain points. I also recruited two more recreational mountain bikers.

TAKEAWAY

The guys in the industry highlighted a few key things. One, riders are looking for more targeted content, especially around caring for their bike. A specific app like this doesn't yet exist, so by creating personalized bike maintenance information in an app has the potential to be extremely valuable to this community. Here is a look at my design overview. It starts with my overall mission for this design and ends with the specific details used in the current iteration.

PAPER WIREFRAMES

After reseearch is complete, it's time to start designing! Here is the initial sketch of the home screen, bike maintenance question, and results page for iOS and Android respectively.

LOW and MID-FIDELITY WIREFRAMES

Once the initial app flows are mapped out on paper, I converted them to Adobe XD to create the low-fidelity wireframes. My priority for this design iteration was to create a personalized questionnaire that provides users with information about their bike and how to service it. Again, it was very important to use standard design practices for gestures, like the toggles you see on the quiz screens below.

USER TESTING

Before I created high-fidelity wireframes, it was important to get feedback. The next thing I did was export my screens to inVision to create quick prototypes. From there, 8 people completed the test. Through this process, I realized my toggle and progress meters were confusing, and they were both designed out in the high-fidelity version. Here are some different versions of the troubleshooting questionnaire I played around with in this phase.

FEEDBACK

Multiple rounds of testing provided the feedback I needed to meet my design objectives, but in the end I think they were achieved; the information hierarchy is clear, the user flows are simple, and there is valuable content for the ideal user.

Final Thoughts

WHAT WORKED

This project taught me a lot about design language. I enjoyed working with Materal Design more than I thought I would! Additionally, I learned a lot about the bike community in Boulder, where I live, and how to plug in better with interviewees.

CHALLENGES FACED

Consistency between the two languages presented a challenge at times, but continuous feedback really helped me get through. I learned to improve my design speed by utilizing UI Kits as well.

NEXT ITERATION

The next round of design will include flows for creating a user profile, adding favorites, and connecting with local mobile bike shops. Looking back at the design strategy I made this project met its design goal, and I look forward to working to complete the big picture goal as well. Thanks for reading.
