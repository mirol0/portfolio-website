Sous Chef

01 - My Design Process

A Big Picture Look At The Design Problem

The big picture with Sous Chef was to join the home-cooking revolution by designing an all-star responsive web app for recipes that helps users in the kitchen and on-the-go. But, to put it simply, there are oodles of options out there for folks to get their recipes, make lists, or find a dinner recipe that's ready in under 30 minutes. My job was to design a tool that makes the whole pocess of deciding what to eat, shoopping for it, and eating it a treat! Sous Chef is a responsive web for recipes, making import easier than ever to have your recipes sync up with all your devices, whether you're out for groceries or stirring the kettle, Sous Chef is there.

02 - Research & Discovery

A Deep Dive Into What's Working v. What's Not.

My hypothesis meant to discover if others found all the text-heavy recipe blogs out there as overwhelming as I do. I didn't want to create just one more tool to bookmark, and eventually forget, about recipes. I wanted to see if there was a way to make the tools people already use more useful. I surveyed 11 users for my research to get a closer look at this frustratingly common issue. Below is a sample question and answer set from the initual user interview survey:

Question: Is there anything you don't like about recipe apps/tools you currently use?

  • Too many options, too many distractions in app, bad quality design or images.

  • Not many ways to organize recipes

  • Nope

  • I wish it would combine with my grocery lists in my iPhone notes. And I hate scrolling so if the recipe and ingredients were better organized that would help.

  • When they have a long blog before the recipe. I hate having to scroll all the way down.

  • Poor layout, too much text.

03 - Wireframes & User Testing

If You Build It (& Keep Testing It) They Will Come

Wih my research complete, I took the information I had and created user personas and user flows in order to help direct the design solution and act as a foundation for the prototypes. Now, at this stage, I started rapid prototyping. Creating quick drawings with pen and paper and uploading them to Marvel allowed me to make edits easily and re-test right away. I received thoughtful feedback from 8 participants on my low-fidelity wireframes, which led to a key improvement. As previously stated, text-heavy blogs are difficult to navigate, so what Sous Chef can do is swipe back and forth from ingredients to instructions, and with the user feedback I received, I added an arrow to make the swipe function clearer.

04 - UI Design Ideation & Testing

Creating That Sous Chef Vibe

Next up: creating a mobile-frst design and a visual identity. The idea behind this strategy is to make the design look good at the smallest platform size and scale it up, so the design looks crisp and consistent at all breakpoints.

The visual identity for Sous Chef is fun and calming, with relaxing shapes. I debated doing a punchier, brighter color scheme, but again with user A/B testing, I decided to stick with the original plan and am very pleased with how it turned out. Below is the style guide for Sous Chef.

06 - Conclusion

Takeaways

This project taught me a lot, and I had a really great, albeit challenging time working on the responsive design aspect. Another major thing I learned was just how random design inspiration can be, and that it is truly important to budget time to just look at the world around you. The idea for the shapes and color scheme for this project came to me based on a book cover I glanced at one day.

Next Iteration

The next steps for this project would be to expand testing and functionality. I would like to focus on the grocery list funcion, and eventually have the tool sync up with other devices, so families can share their grocery lists.
